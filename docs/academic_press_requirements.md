# Academic Press Requirements for the St. Bonaventure Reliquary Book

## Target category

This project should be positioned as a scholarly monograph in Catholic theology, material religion, church history, devotional culture, and archival cataloging. It should not be presented as a simple parish souvenir book unless the target publisher is explicitly devotional or local-history oriented.

## Core book claim

The book must make an argument, not merely present an inventory. The working argument:

> The St. Bonaventure reliquary functions as a local Catholic archive of universal sanctity: a curated material witness to martyrdom, religious orders, ecclesial memory, intercession, and the communion of saints. Its theological significance emerges through the disciplined cataloging of physical display, theca inscriptions, authentication evidence, and historical identity.

## Academic press threshold

A credible academic press submission must show:

- A defined scholarly intervention.
- A clearly bounded corpus: the reliquary and its 200+ relics.
- Evidence discipline: image record, transcription protocol, authentication register, and source hierarchy.
- A theological framework rooted in Catholic doctrine and historical practice.
- Historical notes that separate documented history from hagiographic tradition.
- A full table of contents.
- One or two polished sample chapters.
- A realistic audience and market statement.
- A permissions plan for images and archival materials.

## Required components of the book proposal

### 1. Title and subtitle

Working title:

`The St. Bonaventure Reliquary: A Catholic Archive of Sanctity, Memory, and Material Devotion`

Alternate scholarly title:

`A Parish Reliquary as Catholic Archive: Relics, Memory, and Material Devotion in the St. Bonaventure Collection`

### 2. Project abstract

The proposal should open with a 250–400 word summary of the corpus, method, argument, audience, and contribution.

### 3. Scholarly contribution

The proposal must explain what the book adds. Possible contribution claims:

- It documents a large Catholic reliquary as a structured archive rather than as isolated devotional objects.
- It models a transparent method for cataloging parish-held relics.
- It joins theology of relic veneration with archival practice and material-culture analysis.
- It preserves a local Catholic collection while situating it within universal Church memory.

### 4. Corpus and evidence base

Required evidence layers:

| Evidence layer | Use |
|---|---|
| Original images | Physical layout, labels, thecae, visual condition |
| Brass labels | Display identity and physical order |
| Theca inscriptions | Internal relic label and abbreviated Latin identifiers |
| Certificates/authentication documents | Relic identity, class, seal, authority, date, provenance |
| Parish/diocesan records | Institutional custody and collection history |
| Scholarly sources | Theology, history, material religion, saint biographies |

### 5. Methodology

The methods chapter must describe:

- Physical case mapping.
- Image-based transcription.
- Theca transcription and abbreviation handling.
- Certificate review protocol.
- Confidence grading.
- Evidence hierarchy.
- Duplicate and composite record handling.
- Rules for avoiding overstatement.

### 6. Chapter outline

The proposal should include chapter summaries of 150–250 words each.

Recommended structure:

1. Introduction: The Reliquary as Archive
2. Catholic Theology of Relics
3. Reliquaries, Thecae, Seals, and Sacred Custody
4. The Physical Installation: Chapel, Cases, and Visual Order
5. Cataloging Method and Evidence Rules
6. Apostles, Martyrs, and the Early Church
7. Popes, Bishops, Doctors, and Teachers
8. Founders, Reformers, Missionaries, and Religious Orders
9. Women Saints, Mystics, Virgin Martyrs, and Lay Witnesses
10. Composite Relics and Communities of Martyrdom
11. St. Bonaventure and Franciscan Memory
12. The Full Catalog
13. Provenance, Authentication, and Open Questions
14. Conclusion: Local Custody of Universal Memory

### 7. Sample chapter

Best first sample chapter:

- For a theology press: Chapter 2, Catholic Theology of Relics.
- For a history or material religion press: Chapter 5, Cataloging Method and Evidence Rules.
- For a Catholic institutional press: Chapter 11, St. Bonaventure and Franciscan Memory, paired with catalog examples.

### 8. Audience and market

Primary audiences:

- Scholars of Catholic theology.
- Church historians.
- Scholars of material religion.
- Catholic archive and museum professionals.
- Clergy and diocesan personnel responsible for relic custody.
- Franciscan studies scholars.

Secondary audiences:

- Catholic educators.
- Parish historians.
- Devotional readers seeking a serious, non-sensational treatment.

### 9. Comparable books

The final proposal needs 5–8 comparable titles. Each title should be verified before inclusion. Comparables should include works on:

- Theology of relics.
- Medieval and early Christian relic veneration.
- Material religion.
- Catholic devotional culture.
- Franciscan history.
- Church archives or sacred objects.

Do not fabricate comparables. Add only verified books with publisher, year, and relevance.

### 10. Image and permissions plan

The proposal must state:

- Who owns the reliquary.
- Who owns the photographs.
- Whether parish, diocesan, or private permission is required.
- Whether images will be printed, digitally hosted, or only referenced.
- Whether faces, parish interiors, or location-sensitive details should be redacted.
- Whether certificates may be reproduced in full or only transcribed.

### 11. Manuscript specifications

Working target:

- 80,000–110,000 words for full scholarly monograph.
- 40–80 figures if the press accepts an illustrated work.
- Full catalog appendix, possibly digital-only if the print book would become too large.
- Chicago notes-bibliography is likely preferable for theology/history; final style depends on press.

### 12. Completion timetable

Proposed timetable after image stabilization:

| Month | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1 | Original image archive, case map, visible catalog complete |
| 2 | Label and theca crops complete; catalog audit complete |
| 3 | Certificate/provenance register initiated |
| 4 | Theological framework chapter drafted |
| 5 | Historical notes for first 50 relics |
| 6 | Historical notes for remaining visible relics |
| 7 | Catalog chapter and appendices drafted |
| 8 | Sample chapter polished; proposal package assembled |
| 9 | Parish/diocesan review and permissions review |
| 10 | Submit press proposal |

### 13. Required before submission

Do not submit to an academic press until these are done:

- Every visible relic has a stable record ID.
- Every visible relic has a label confidence grade.
- Every image in the repository has checksum metadata.
- Every theological claim is sourced.
- Every authentication claim is tied to certificate/provenance evidence.
- Image permissions are resolved.
- One sample chapter is polished.
- Proposal has a clear market and comparable-books section.

## Recommended press categories

Potential categories to investigate after the proposal package matures:

- Catholic university press.
- Theology and religious studies academic press.
- Franciscan studies publisher.
- Church history publisher.
- Museum/material-culture publisher.
- Catholic devotional publisher for a shorter illustrated version.

Final press selection should be made only after the manuscript's dominant identity is clear: theology, Catholic history, material religion, Franciscan studies, or illustrated parish archive.
