# Book Proposal Draft

## Working title

`The St. Bonaventure Reliquary: A Catholic Archive of Sanctity, Memory, and Material Devotion`

## Alternate title

`A Parish Reliquary as Catholic Archive: Relics, Memory, and Material Devotion in the St. Bonaventure Collection`

## Project abstract

This book documents and interprets a Catholic reliquary associated with St. Bonaventure and containing more than 200 relics displayed in multiple illuminated wall cases. The collection includes saints, martyrs, popes, founders, missionaries, Doctors of the Church, mystics, and composite martyr groupings. Rather than treating the reliquary as a devotional curiosity, the book approaches it as a local Catholic archive of universal sanctity: a structured material witness to the communion of saints, ecclesial memory, bodily holiness, martyrdom, and sacred custody.

The project combines theological interpretation with archival cataloging. It begins with physical mapping of the reliquary cases, exact transcription of visible brass labels, theca-level inspection, and a controlled authentication register. Each relic is cataloged through separate evidence layers: display label, theca inscription, certificate or provenance record, historical identity, and theological significance. This structure prevents overstatement while allowing the collection to be studied as a coherent theological and historical object.

The book argues that parish-held reliquaries preserve more than devotional fragments. They embody Catholic memory in material form. They gather distant saints into local custody, join universal Church history to a specific community, and invite disciplined reflection on sanctity, intercession, and the body within Catholic tradition. The St. Bonaventure reliquary is therefore valuable not only as a collection of relics, but also as a case study in how Catholic communities receive, preserve, display, and interpret sacred material inheritance.

## Core argument

The St. Bonaventure reliquary functions as a Catholic archive of sanctity. Its theological meaning arises from the relationship among relic, label, case, certificate, saintly memory, and local custody. The collection should be studied through disciplined cataloging before devotional, historical, or theological synthesis is attempted.

## Scholarly contribution

This project contributes to theology, Catholic history, material religion, and archival practice by:

- Documenting a large Catholic reliquary through a transparent evidence hierarchy.
- Modeling a cataloging method for parish-held relic collections.
- Connecting relic veneration to Catholic teaching on sanctity, intercession, and communion.
- Treating the reliquary as a material archive rather than a mere decorative or devotional display.
- Preserving local Catholic heritage while situating it in the wider history of the Church.

## Evidence base

The initial evidence base includes 36 photographs of the reliquary installation and visible labels. The next evidence layer will require cropped label images, cropped theca images, certificate scans, seal documentation, and parish or diocesan provenance records.

The manuscript will distinguish:

- Physical display evidence.
- Brass label evidence.
- Theca inscription evidence.
- Certificate/provenance evidence.
- Historical scholarship.
- Theological interpretation.

## Proposed table of contents

### 1. Introduction: The Reliquary as Archive

Introduces the St. Bonaventure reliquary as a physical, theological, and archival object. Defines the problem: Catholic relic collections are often preserved devotionally but not cataloged with sufficient scholarly discipline. Establishes the book's method and argument.

### 2. Catholic Theology of Relics

Explains the Catholic theology of relic veneration, sanctity, bodily holiness, intercession, and the communion of saints. Distinguishes veneration from worship and situates relics within Catholic sacramental and devotional imagination.

### 3. Reliquaries, Thecae, Seals, and Sacred Custody

Describes the material culture of relic preservation: thecae, reliquaries, seals, labels, certificates, custody, and ecclesial responsibility. Establishes why documentation matters theologically and historically.

### 4. The Physical Installation: Chapel, Cases, and Visual Order

Maps the wall-mounted reliquary cases, red backing, brass labels, and visual sequence. Explains the apparent alphabetic organization and the importance of preserving physical order before interpretation.

### 5. Cataloging Method and Evidence Rules

Presents the cataloging protocol: record IDs, section IDs, transcription rules, theca inscription fields, confidence grades, verification statuses, and provenance controls.

### 6. Apostles, Martyrs, and the Early Church

Interprets saints and groups associated with early Christian witness, martyrdom, apostolic memory, and foundational Church identity.

### 7. Popes, Bishops, Doctors, and Teachers

Examines relics of popes, bishops, Doctors, and theologians as witnesses to teaching authority, reform, pastoral governance, and doctrinal memory.

### 8. Founders, Reformers, Missionaries, and Religious Orders

Examines saints connected to religious orders, missions, evangelization, reform movements, and institutional charisms.

### 9. Women Saints, Mystics, Virgin Martyrs, and Lay Witnesses

Interprets the collection's witness to women saints, mystical life, virgin martyrdom, lay sanctity, and family or domestic holiness.

### 10. Composite Relics and Communities of Martyrdom

Studies grouped relic labels such as Cecilia, Valerian, and Tiburtius; Canadian Martyrs; Jacinta and Francisco Marto; and other composite devotional records.

### 11. St. Bonaventure and Franciscan Memory

Places the St. Bonaventure relic within Franciscan theology, memory, learning, poverty, mystical ascent, and the intellectual tradition of the Church.

### 12. The Full Catalog

Presents the catalog of visible relics, organized by physical section and record ID, with notes on label transcription, normalized saint names, evidence status, and research needs.

### 13. Provenance, Authentication, and Open Questions

Reviews certificate evidence, unresolved readings, variant spellings, duplicate labels, and the limits of image-based identification.

### 14. Conclusion: Local Custody of Universal Memory

Synthesizes the reliquary as a local act of custody over universal Catholic memory and proposes a model for future parish reliquary documentation.
